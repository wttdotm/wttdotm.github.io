🥇 Webby Award Winner, 2025

Traffic Cam Photobooth is my ongoing project of using traffic cameras as creative medium. Over the course of its development, it has garnered international press, gallery exhibitions, and even won a Webby Award. It started with the original Traffic Cam Photobooth website, which was a site that allowed anybody to locate their nearest publicly available traffic camera and use it to take a selfie. This was followed by the threat of legal action from the city in the form of a Cease & Desist letter, which I then used as the inspiration and material for a follow-up piece where I used the traffic cameras to scan the very same demand letter. In 2026, I created GardenCam, a website that streamed the traffic cameras outside of Madison Square Garden for the duration of the Knicks vs. Spurs finals series. GardenCam also followed in its forebears footsteps by once again accidentally becoming political, as along with the revelry outside of MSG, GardenCam also caught the unprecedented lockdown of Midtown Manhattan as Donald Trump's visit to game three imposed a strict perimeter across a large swath of the city. Description

Traffic Cam Photobooth is a website that allows anybody to locate their nearest publicly available traffic camera and use it to take pictures of themselves. While these cameras are ostensibly intended for traffic, they also serve to acclimate us to the idea that constant monitoring is an everyday part life. No matter the target of this surveillance, it's clear from looking at the map that most New Yorkers (and since its launch, now people in many other locations) get unconsentingly captured by the lens of at least one camera - if not several - every day. TCP offers visitors an engaging and lighthearted way to engage with this very serious topic by drawing attention to these easily ignored cameras. People can use their feeds, which their tax dollars help fund, to take pictures of themselves, spreading the knowledge of this sprawling surveillance apparatus through fun self-portraits designed to be sharable online.

Cease & Desist Project

Photography
2024

On November 7th, 2024, the New York City Department of Transportation sent me a Cease & Desist letter demanding I take down the Traffic Cam Photobooth website. In response, I took the letter, mounted it onto a 25 foot long window washing pole, went to a traffic camera in downtown Brooklyn, and took a picture of the Cease & Desist with the exact infrastructure the city was demanding I stop using. I then took the same pole to a traffic camera right outside NYPD Times Square station, replaced the letter with a mirror, and gave a camera that had been used for so much surveillance and citizen selfies to, for once, take its own selfie. The two resulting photos, along with some process pictures, were exhibited at the MUD Foundation's 2024 Art Week show down in Miami.

GardenCam

Website, Video
2026

GardenCam was a website made for the Knicks 2026 championship run that streamed the traffic cameras outside of Madison Square Garden for the duration of the Knicks vs. Spurs finals series. Initially created in order to capture the reverie outside of MSG, GardenCam also inadvertently caught the imposition of one of the strictest lockdowns midtown manhattan had seen in decades as Donald Trump visited for Game 3 and the resulting perimeter lasted through Game 4. Upon the Knicks clinching the chip in Game 5, GardenCam also caught exclusive footage of celebrations around the city and the resulting parade later that week.
